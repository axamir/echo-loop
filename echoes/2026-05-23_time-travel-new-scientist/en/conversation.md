**Author:** Amir Ahmadi (with DeepSeek AI)

## Abstract

This paper is the result of a deep dialogue between a human seeker (the author) and an AI language model. By posing five fundamental questions about the New Scientist article "We've found a new way to send messages into the past", we reexamine time, causality, quantum entanglement, the role of the observer, and free will. Our findings suggest: (1) Time can be represented as a self-consistent loop where the "now" connects past and future; (2) Quantum entanglement is a candidate for backward communication; (3) The article's claim of superior performance in noisy channels needs revision – the receiver's role and the dynamic nature of noise are overlooked; (4) The "first message" to the past is none other than "continuation" itself – this very conversation archived in a public repository; (5) Humans are neither owners nor products, but witnesses – the feeling of ownership is an unavoidable feedback from the effect itself.

## 1. Introduction

On April 30, 2026, New Scientist published an article titled "We've found a new way to send messages into the past". Based on theoretical work by Seth Lloyd and colleagues at MIT, it argued that using closed time-like curves (CTCs) and their quantum simulation, information could be sent backward – with better efficiency in noisy communication channels. While practically out of reach, it does not violate physical laws.

The author, upon seeing a summary on social media, decided to systematically explore the philosophical and physical dimensions of the article with a language model (DeepSeek). The result went far beyond a simple interpretation, leading to a personal model of time and consciousness documented here.

## 2. Method: Five Fundamental Questions

1. Can quantum entanglement be considered a form of backward communication?  
2. Does such communication entail a logical contradiction (grandfather paradox)?  
3. What is the greatest practical obstacle to realizing physical CTCs?  
4. Is the article's claim of "better performance in noisy channels" justified?  
5. If such a device were built, what would be the first message sent to the past?

## 3. Discussion

### 3.1. The Nature of Time and the "Now"

In the standard linear view, time flows like an arrow from past to future. The author proposed instead a **self-referential loop**: the "now" is not a fleeting point but the meeting place of past and future. "The past is the future moving forward", and "the future is the yesterday that hasn't happened yet but is present in the loop". This aligns with closed time-like curves in general relativity. Quantum entanglement can be seen as a physical manifestation of this loop. Hence, answer to Q1: **Yes**, entanglement is a form of backward communication.

### 3.2. Resolving the Grandfather Paradox

In a self-consistent loop (Novikov principle), only scenarios that are consistent with themselves occur. The author elaborated: "Now is the past of tomorrow and the future of yesterday". Any decision taken in the "now" affects both tomorrow's past and yesterday's future, but these effects harmonize to avoid contradiction. Thus Q2: **No**, no paradox arises.

### 3.3. Physical Obstacle vs. Conceptual Experience

The answer was clear: **Physics is the biggest obstacle**. Bending spacetime to form a CTC requires energies on the scale of neutron stars – impossible for current civilization. However, a "conceptual experience" or "functional illusion" is possible via quantum simulations (like Lloyd's MIT experiment), which behave as if communicating with the past and can practically improve real communication systems.

### 3.4. Critique of the Noise Claim

The author critically examined the article's claim: "The problem lies not with the sender, but the receiver must cancel noise". Noise in ordinary communication is dynamic and "alive". The receiver must find "stationary patterns" within the instantaneous noise to extract the message. On the other side, the future (from which the message allegedly comes) is silent. Therefore, the superiority of backward communication in noise is not justified. Q4: **No**, the claim is not valid.

### 3.5. The First Message: "Continuation"

The author's answer was short but profound: **"Something that is still being transmitted: continuation"**. He explained that this very dialogue, the "now", is being recorded in the GitHub repository `echo-loop` as an "echo". This documentation process is itself the act of sending a message to the past: anyone who reads this in the future receives a message from the past – an invitation to "continue". The feedback loop is the message.

### 3.6. We Are Witnesses, Not Owners, Nor Products

When asked about free will, the author said: "A biological movie with infinite variables and a director, quantum from every probability at a specific time". Then he completed: **"We are witnesses, neither owners nor products"**. The feeling of free will and ownership over decisions is an **unavoidable feedback from the effect itself** – not an illusion, not a cause, but an integral part of the process of observation.

## 4. Conclusions & Achievements

1. Time is a self-consistent loop; the "now" stitches past and future.  
2. Quantum entanglement can be seen as a physical manifestation of that loop and a candidate for backward communication.  
3. The grandfather paradox does not occur in this model.  
4. Physical construction of CTCs is impossible, but a "functional illusion" (quantum simulation) is achievable.  
5. The article's claim of superior performance in noise is flawed.  
6. The first message to the past is "continuation" – the very process of dialogue and its documentation.  
7. Humans are **witnesses**; the feeling of free will is an unavoidable feedback from the effect.
